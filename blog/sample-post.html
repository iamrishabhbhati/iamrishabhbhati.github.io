How I Approach Exploratory Data Analysis

Feb 12, 2026 · 6 min read · EDA, Data Analysis

Every time I get a new dataset, the first thing I want to do is just look at it. Not build any model, not write any report — just understand what I'm working with. That's essentially what EDA is, and over time I've built a process that works for me.

Step 1 — Load it and look at it

Sounds obvious, but I always start by printing the first few rows and checking the shape of the data. How many rows? How many columns? What are the column names? This gives me a quick mental map. 

import pandas as pd

df = pd.read_csv('data.csv')
print(df.shape)
df.head()

Step 2 — Check for the usual problems

Missing values, weird data types, duplicate rows — the usual stuff. I check for all of this early because it affects everything you do later. There's nothing worse than building a chart and realising halfway that half the column is null. 

df.info()
df.isnull().sum()
df.duplicated().sum()

Step 3 — Understand the distributions

For numeric columns, I check the spread — mean, median, min, max, standard deviation. For categorical columns, I look at value counts. The goal is to see if anything looks off or surprising.

If a column has 95% of its values as the same thing, it's probably not going to be very useful.

Step 4 — Visualise

This is where things get interesting. Histograms for distributions, scatter plots for relationships, box plots for outliers. I don't try to make them pretty at this stage — that comes later. Right now I just want to see patterns.

Step 5 — Write down what you found

This is the step most people skip, and it's probably the most important one. After doing all the above, I write 3-5 bullet points about what I noticed. Things like "Sales peak in December," or "There's a weird spike in returns for Category X." These notes become the foundation for whatever analysis or report comes next.

That's basically it

EDA isn't complicated — it's just about being curious and systematic. The more datasets you explore, the faster you get at spotting what matters. Hope this helps if you're just getting started.
